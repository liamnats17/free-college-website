Why College Should Be Free

Free college education has the potential to transform society by increasing accessibility to higher education, reducing student debt, and boosting economic growth.

  • Increased Accessibility: Free tuition eliminates barriers for low-income and marginalized students, creating a more equitable society. For instance, the average combined cost of undergraduate tuition, fees, room, and board at four-year institutions has doubled since 2000, making higher education less accessible to low-income students.
  • Reduced Debt: Graduates with less debt can contribute more to the economy, purchase homes, and save for the future. Currently, the total student loan debt in the U.S. has reached $1.73 trillion, significantly impacting graduates' financial stability.
  • Economic Growth: An educated workforce generates higher tax revenue and reduces reliance on public assistance. Four-year college graduates earn 31% more annually than those with only high school diplomas, contributing an additional $1.2 trillion to the economy over 10 years.
"According to College Board research, four-year college graduates paid, on average, 82% more in taxes than high school graduates." — Wingspan Staff

Addressing the Opposition

Critics argue that free college might lower educational quality or strain public finances. Here’s why these fears are unfounded:

  • Taxpayer Burden: Implementing free college programs can lead to increased tax revenues that offset the initial public investment. For example, the annual benefits of a free-college program would exceed the annual costs within a decade.
  • Student Motivation: Free college initiatives can enhance student motivation and enrollment. A randomized control trial in Milwaukee Public Schools found that the availability of free college funds increased students' motivation and steps toward college enrollment.
  • Infrastructure Strain: Concerns about infrastructure strain due to increased enrollment can be managed with strategic planning. The Brookings Institution notes that the design of free college programs is crucial, and proper implementation can mitigate potential issues related to infrastructure and capacity.
"Free college sounds simple enough—but in reality, it involves many choices tied to different interpretations of the word ‘free.’" — Anthony P. Carnevale, et al.
